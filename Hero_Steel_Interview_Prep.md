## PART 0 — THE DATASET & TECHNOLOGY EXPLAINED

Before your video call, here is a simple, plain-English breakdown of what the data represents, how it was handled, and what technology makes the webpage tick.

### 1. What is in this Dataset?
The dataset contains **35,040 rows**, which represents electrical readings taken every **15 minutes for a full 365-day year** (2018) from the DAEWOO Steel Co. plant in South Korea. Here is what the columns actually mean:
*   **Active Energy (`Usage_kWh`)**: This is the actual usable power consumed by the plant's machinery (like heavy industrial equipment and rolling operations). This is the primary driver of the plant's electricity bill.
*   **Reactive Power (`Lagging` & `Leading` `kVarh`)**: This is "non-working" or magnetizing power. Heavy industrial equipment like motor windings and induction furnaces require this power to create magnetic fields to operate. Think of it like the foam on a mug of beer: you need it to pour the beer, but you only want to pay for the liquid (Active Power).
*   **Power Factor (`Lagging` & `Leading` `PF`)**: This is a score from `0.0` to `1.0` representing electrical efficiency. It measures how effectively the plant converts incoming power into active work. A perfect score is `1.0`. If the Power Factor falls below `0.90`, the utility company charges massive financial penalty surcharges because poor power factor strains the power grid.
*   **CO2 Emissions (`CO2_tCO2`)**: A calculated estimate of the carbon footprint generated by the plant’s active power consumption, crucial for corporate ESG (Environmental, Social, and Governance) targets.
*   **Load Type**: Grouped into `Light_Load`, `Medium_Load`, and `Maximum_Load` based on the level of production activity.

### 2. Did we Clean or Modify the Dataset?
Yes, we performed crucial data cleaning and **Feature Engineering** (creating new calculated columns that weren't in the raw data) to make the dataset production-ready:
*   **Apparent Power (`Apparent_Power_kVA`)**: We calculated the actual total electrical demand drawn from the grid using the geometric sum of active and reactive power: $\sqrt{\text{kWh}^2 + \text{kVArh}^2}$.
*   **Production Shifts (`shift`)**: We engineered a column grouping hours into 4 plant shifts: *Night (00:00-05:00)*, *Morning (06:00-13:00)*, *Afternoon (14:00-21:00)*, and *Evening (22:00-23:00)*. This allows operations to see which crew or shift is consuming energy inefficiently.
*   **Rolling Statistics**: We added calculated 1-hour rolling averages to smooth out minor fluctuations and highlight sustained power surges.
*   **Z-Scores**: We calculated how far each active energy reading deviates from the plant's overall yearly average, allowing our system to spot massive demand spikes instantly.
*   **Cleaning**: We ensured all dates were correctly parsed, checked for missing or corrupted values, programmatically handled outliers, and stored the clean set in `steel_energy.csv`.

### 3. What is Streamlit and What is Happening on the Webpage?
*   **What is Streamlit?** Streamlit is a modern, open-source Python framework that allows developers to turn Python scripts into interactive, highly responsive web applications instantly, completely eliminating the need to write complex front-end code (HTML, CSS, or JavaScript).
*   **How does the webpage work?** 
    1.  **Loading**: When you open the web page, Streamlit executes our Python code (`app.py`). It reads the 35,040 rows from the CSV via `data_loader.py`.
    2.  **Caching**: Because processing 35,000+ rows takes time, we used Streamlit's `@st.cache_data` feature. This loads and transforms the dataset once, storing it in server memory so the dashboard stays lightning-fast when you click filters.
    3.  **ML Inference**: It loads and trains the **Isolation Forest** model (for finding anomalies) and the **Random Forest** model (for classifying loads) on startup.
    4.  **Interactivity**: When you select a month (e.g., "March") or toggle a slider in the sidebar, Streamlit instantly intercepts that action, filters the dataset in the background, recalculates the equal-height KPI cards, and updates all interactive Plotly charts dynamically.
    5.  **Plotly Engine**: The charts are built using Plotly, which means they aren't static images—you can hover over any point to read exact coordinates, zoom in on specific hours, or toggle legend items to hide/show lines.

### FROM THE OPERATIONS HEAD

---

**Q: How does Isolation Forest actually work? Why not use a threshold-based alarm?**

> *"Threshold alarms — like 'flag anything above 25 kWh' — are simple but brittle. They generate too many false positives during legitimate peak production, and they miss correlated failures where individual readings look normal but the combination is abnormal. Isolation Forest builds a random tree ensemble and measures how quickly a point gets isolated. Points that are easy to isolate — they're unusual across multiple dimensions simultaneously — get high anomaly scores. So it catches scenarios like: normal kWh reading but abnormal reactive power AND elevated CO2 together. A threshold would miss that entirely."*

---

**Q: The dataset is from a South Korean plant — how relevant is it to our operations?**

> *"The schema — Usage kWh, reactive power, power factor, CO2, load classification — is standard for any arc furnace or induction furnace-based plant. The physical relationships between these variables are governed by electrical laws, not geography. That said, I'd calibrate the contamination parameter and retrain the model on Hero Steel's actual SCADA output within the first month. The architecture is production-ready for that."*

---

**Q: What's the cost of a false positive — flagging something as anomalous that isn't?**

> *"That's a really important question that I thought about. At a 1.2% setting, we get about 1 flag per day. I know that if I send a maintenance crew on a wild goose chase every day, they'll stop trusting the system. But missing a real transformer failure is much worse. If I were deploying this for real, I would want to sit down with the maintenance team, start the sensitivity very strictly so they only see the most obvious faults, and then tune it together based on their feedback."*

---

**Q: Power factor of 0.917 — that's above 0.90. Where's the real problem?**

> *"The average is 0.917, but averages hide the tail. Over 35% of individual 15-minute readings were below 0.90. Utility penalty clauses are typically triggered by monthly averages or peak-hour averages — not the full-year mean. The risk is concentrated in peak production hours when reactive power demand from induction motors and arc furnaces spikes. That's exactly the band we need to correct with capacitor banks — not everywhere, just at the HT bus during peak hours."*

---

**Q: Can this system handle real-time data, or is it just historical analysis?**

> *"Currently it runs on historical batch data — a CSV loaded at startup. Making it real-time requires two changes: first, a data pipeline that reads from your SCADA or historian (OSIsoft PI, for example) at 15-minute intervals; second, the anomaly model runs on a rolling window rather than the full dataset. Streamlit supports live data refresh with `st.rerun`. The ML models themselves are stateless — they can score new readings in milliseconds. The dashboard architecture was designed with that upgrade path in mind."*

---

**Q: The load classifier gets 99% accuracy — isn't that suspiciously high?**

> *"Good catch — it would be suspicious if the classes weren't separable. But load type in this dataset is essentially a deterministic function of energy draw — Light under 4.5 kWh, Medium between 4.5 and 11, Maximum above that. So the classifier is learning a near-deterministic boundary. The value isn't in the classification itself — it's in predicting FUTURE load type from power quality features alone, 15 minutes before the actual draw registers. That's where the business case lives, and I'd validate it on a rolling prediction task with Hero Steel's historical data."*
